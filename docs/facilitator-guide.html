Camp AIR: AI Agent Evaluation Workshop

Facilitator Guide — Delivery & Human Side

1. Purpose of This Guide

This guide is a companion to session-outline.md, which already covers segment-by-segment talking points, timing notes, demo scripts, and common Q&A. Do not duplicate those materials here.

What this guide covers instead: the human side of running the session. How to read thirty people in a room, how to handle the participant who wants to argue about methodology, how to recover when something breaks, and how to finish with energy rather than fizzling out. Use both documents together. The outline tells you what to say. This guide tells you how to be in the room.

2. Facilitator Profile and Mindset

Who should run this session: - Someone who has written and run evals in practice — not just read about them. Participants will ask questions that only experience can answer. - A PM or engineer with firsthand AI agent work. Serena's profile is ideal: real product context, real eval failures, real lessons learned. - Comfortable with TypeScript at a reading level. You do not need to write code live, but you need to not panic when a participant shows you a stack trace.

What experience you do NOT need: - A background in ML or formal evaluation methodology - Any specific eval framework beyond the harness in this repo - Teaching or facilitation credentials

The right mental posture:

You are a coach, not a lecturer. The goal of this session is not for participants to absorb everything you know. It is for each person to leave having run a real eval once, with enough vocabulary to keep going on their own. The best thing you can do is create conditions for that — clear instructions, a room where it is safe to ask dumb questions, and a facilitator who does not dominate the airtime.

Concretely: - Ask questions, do not just answer them. "What do you think the grader should check here?" is more valuable than giving the answer. - Resist the urge to over-explain. If a concept lands, move on. Participants who want more depth have /eval-faq and the repo. - Tolerate mess. During the hands-on section, the room will look chaotic. That is the right state. Do not rush to restore order.

3. Quick-Reference Timing Card

Print this and keep it on the podium or tape it to your laptop bezel.

Segment Clock Time What You Should Be Doing Escape Hatch if Running Behind
Opening / Framing 0:00 – 0:05 Hook question, why evals matter, set agenda Cut to the agenda immediately after the hook — skip elaboration
Core Concepts 0:05 – 0:15 Walk through 5 terms, one Q&A pause Drop the Q&A pause, go straight to demo transition
Live Demo 0:15 – 0:25 Demo skills pipeline + show synthetic results Walk through the synthetic CSV instead of live demo
Hands-On Exercise 0:25 – 0:50 Rove the room, check in at ~0:40 Tell participants to copy the generated test set as-is rather than perfecting it
Debrief & Wrap-Up 0:50 – 1:00 Call on 3–4 people, read takeaways, closing line Cut to 2 takeaways + closing line only if very short on time

Hard stops: You must start the hands-on no later than 0:28. You must start the debrief no later than 0:52. Everything else is negotiable.

4. Demo Preparation Checklist

The demo in Segment 3 is the moment the whole session pivots on. If it goes well, participants go into the hands-on with confidence. If it breaks, you spend the first five minutes of hands-on time doing damage control.

48 hours before the session: - [ ] Clone a fresh copy of the eval harness repo - [ ] Install Claude Code skills (/eval-generator, /eval-result-interpreter, /eval-faq) and confirm they load without errors - [ ] Run /eval-generator in Claude Code with the exact customer support prompt from the outline. Confirm it produces usable scenarios - [ ] Have synthetic-eval-results.csv ready to share/display — this is the pre-built results file participants will use for result interpretation

Morning of the session: - [ ] Run the skills pipeline one more time on the actual laptop you will present from - [ ] Set your terminal font size to at least 18pt — participants in the back need to read it - [ ] Have synthetic-eval-results.csv open and one keystroke away (separate tab or terminal pane) - [ ] Disable notifications and close Slack

Backup plan if the skills demo fails mid-demo: Say: "Let me switch to the prepared results — this is the same synthetic CSV you'll be using in the exercise." Open synthetic-eval-results.csv and continue narrating. The synthetic CSV is always the fallback. Do not apologize more than once.

5. Reading the Room

Room is quiet or disengaged

Signal: No hands go up after the opening hook. Minimal eye contact. People looking at laptops before the hands-on starts.

Response: Do not panic and start talking faster. Quiet does not always mean disengaged — this audience is engineers and PMs who think before they speak.

What to do: Ask a more specific question. Instead of "any questions?", try "What's the worst silent failure you've seen from an AI feature in production?" Give it five full seconds of silence before you move on. If no response, move on without comment — do not editorialize about the lack of response.

During Core Concepts: call on someone by name if you know them. "Hey, does that distinction between criteria and grader make sense to you?" Warmth beats force.

Room is moving too slow during hands-on

Signal: At your mid-Stage-B check-in (~0:40), fewer than half have raised hands confirming they have a dataset file saved.

Response: Immediately give a simplification directive. Walk to the center of the room and say:

"Here's a shortcut — if you haven't finished generating yet, copy the generator output as-is, don't perfect it. A complete but imperfect test set is better than no test set. Move on to Stage C."

Extend Stage B by 3–4 minutes and compress Stage C to: "Run it once, capture whatever output appears, bring that to the debrief." A partial result is a valid result.

Room is moving fast — early finishers

Signal: A few people finish their eval file before 0:42 and are clearly waiting.

Response: Have a stretch prompt ready. You can display it or just say it aloud when you notice them:

"If you've run your eval and have results, add a second grader — if you have only a deterministic grader, add a model-graded one, or vice versa. Then re-run and see if the pass/fail pattern changes. Also: delete your three easiest cases and replace them with harder ones."

This keeps them engaged and models the right habit — eval suites grow by adding harder cases, not by declaring them done.

Strong side conversation developing

Signal: Two or three participants are having a deep technical exchange that is pulling attention from others, or they are going deep on something (model-graded grader implementation, CI setup, etc.) that is outside the session scope.

Response: Acknowledge and redirect, do not shut down. Walk over and say: "This sounds like a good thread — I want to make sure we capture it. Can you two keep a note and bring it to the debrief?" Then physically move away, which usually breaks the gravity of the side conversation.

If the topic is actually interesting to the whole room, you can choose to surface it: "Actually, this question about [topic] might be worth 60 seconds for the group." Then give it 60 seconds and move on explicitly.

Technical failure — npm error, auth issue, etc.

Signal: A participant (or multiple) has an error they cannot get past in the first 5 minutes of Stage A or B.

Response: Triage fast. The two most common issues: 1. Auth error on /eval-faq or /eval-generator: Have them re-run claude auth login in a new terminal. This is usually a session token issue. 2. "Skill not found" error: Have them restart Claude Code entirely. Skills sometimes fail to load on first launch.

If more than 5 people have blocking errors: stop the room briefly, acknowledge it ("Looks like a few of us have a setup issue — let's spend 2 minutes getting everyone unblocked"), and walk through the fix for the most common cause. Do not let one setup problem quietly strand a third of the room.

6. Participant Archetypes

The Skeptic

How to spot them: "Couldn't you just... run it manually and see if it looks right?" or "This seems like a lot of infrastructure for a chatbot."

What they need: Acknowledgment before reframe. Do not argue. Say: "That works at small scale — the question is what happens when you change the prompt and you have 50 cases to check. Or when you have three team members each making changes." Concrete scale is the best reframe. If they remain skeptical, that is fine. They are engaging, not checked out.

The Perfectionist

How to spot them: Fifteen minutes into Stage B, they have not run a single command. They are still writing criteria in a doc, or rewriting the prompt template, or asking eval-faq five follow-up questions.

What they need: A forcing function. Walk over and say, directly but warmly: "Copy your generator output and move to Stage C. An imperfect test set that gets interpreted is worth more than a perfect one that never leaves the generator. You can fix the criteria after you see the results." If they push back, tell them the goal for this session is a complete test set plus result interpretation — not one perfect eval.

The Explorer

How to spot them: Deep in the repo, exploring /eval-faq deeply, or trying to add a custom grader type, or Googling something unrelated to the task.

What they need: A gentle boundary and a follow-up offer. Say: "I love that you're going deep — hold that thread, finish the core exercise, and come find me after the session. I want to hear what you found." Give them the satisfaction of knowing their rabbit hole is valued, but redirect them to the shared outcome.

The Disengaged

How to spot them: Phone out, no editor open, not writing anything during Stage B.

What they need: A direct but low-stakes invitation. Walk over during the hands-on and ask quietly: "Which of the four agent tasks looks most relevant to what you're working on right now?" If they pick one, that is your opening — help them get started on that specific task. If they do not engage, do not force it. Some people learn by watching.

Do not call them out publicly. That creates embarrassment without creating engagement.

The Expert

How to spot them: Asks pointed methodology questions during Core Concepts ("What's your position on using the same model family for grader and system-under-test?"), or is already running an eval framework at their company.

What they need: To be used, not just accommodated. During the hands-on, loop back to them: "You've probably thought about this more than most — I'd love you to share your setup in the debrief. Even if it's different from what we're doing here, the contrast will be useful for the group." This channels their energy toward the session rather than against it.

7. Facilitation Scripts for Transitions

These are the handoff lines between segments. Exact wording matters here — a clunky transition breaks momentum.

Opening → Core Concepts (at ~0:05):

"Let's get grounded in the vocabulary first, then I'll show you a working example."

Core Concepts → Live Demo (at ~0:15):

"Alright — let's stop talking about evals and look at one actually running."

Live Demo → Hands-On (at ~0:25):

"Now it's your turn. You're going to write and run your own eval in the next 25 minutes. Instructions are on screen — let's go."

Stage A → Stage B (at ~0:30):

"Wrap up your eval-faq questions — we're moving to scenario generation. Pick your agent task from the slide and open eval-generator."

Stage B check-in (at ~0:40):

"Quick check-in — raise your hand if you've got your dataset file saved and you're working on the eval file now."

Stage B → Stage C (at ~0:45):

"Now let's interpret results — open the synthetic CSV I shared and run /eval-result-interpreter. Answer the three questions on the slide — you'll share in debrief."

Stage C → Debrief (at ~0:50):

"One more minute — wrap up what you're looking at, we're going to share results." (pause 60 seconds) "Okay, let's come back together. Who wants to go first — what did you build, and what did your results tell you?"

8. Handling Q&A During Core Concepts

The Core Concepts segment is 10 minutes covering 5 terms. That is tight. Questions are welcome, but one good question can eat three minutes if you are not deliberate.

Strategies:

  • The "park it" move: When a question is good but would take more than 90 seconds to answer, say "That's exactly the right question — let's see if the demo makes it concrete, and if not, we'll come back to it." Then actually come back to it, or flag it for the debrief.
  • Use the FAQ tool as a redirect: For questions about edge cases or implementation details, say: "That's a great one for eval-faq — ask it that exact question during Stage A and let me know what it says." This is not a deflection; it models how participants should use the tool.
  • One question, one answer: After answering a question during this segment, do not open the floor again. Say: "Good — let's keep moving" and return to the next concept.
  • The question that is really a statement: Some participants phrase opinions as questions ("Isn't model-graded scoring kind of circular though?"). Acknowledge the point, give a 20-second response, and move on. Do not let it become a debate.

The Q&A pause after all five concepts is intentional. That is the right time for genuine clarification questions. Before that, questions should be brief or parked.

9. Debrief Facilitation Techniques

Cold-calling without embarrassing people

Do not just ask "who wants to share?" — silence follows and then only the most extroverted participant speaks. Instead:

  • Before the debrief, note 2–3 people during the hands-on whose work looked interesting (either notably good results, or an eval that failed in an instructive way). Ask them first.
  • Frame the ask in a low-stakes way: "I was watching [Name]'s screen during the exercise and saw something interesting — [Name], can you show us your results? Even if they're partial."
  • Give an out: "And if your eval errored out completely, that's actually worth sharing too — we'll make it useful."

When a participant's generator didn't produce useful output

Do not skip them. A generator that produced weak output is the richest teaching case. Say:

"Walk us through what happened. What did the generator give you? What would you change about the prompt?"

Then turn it to the group: "Anyone recognize that issue? What would you adjust?" This turns a weak result into a prompt-refinement exercise, which is both useful and normalizing — real eval design is iterative, and improving prompts is the job.

Surfacing the key lesson from whatever results the room produced

The room will produce varied results — some passing evals, some failing ones, some errors. Regardless of what comes up, these are the three lessons you can anchor to in any debrief:

  1. If most things passed: "Evals that always pass are not testing the right things yet. What's the hardest case your agent will face in production? Is it in your dataset?"
  2. If many things failed: "Failures are information. The question is: is the eval wrong, or is the model wrong? Walk me through one failure and let's figure out which one it is."
  3. If the AI-generated scenarios came up: "How many of you changed or deleted at least one scenario the generator gave you? That's the right instinct. The tool drafts; you decide."

10. Energy and Pacing Notes

A 1-hour session has a predictable energy arc. Know it so you can work with it.

0:00 – 0:10 (Rising): Energy comes from novelty and the hook question. Participants are paying attention. This is your window to land the framing. Do not waste it on logistics.

0:10 – 0:25 (Plateau): Core concepts and demo. Energy is steady but passive — people are watching, not doing. Keep your own energy up here, because you are the energy source. Move around if you can. Use the whiteboard. Do not stand behind the podium.

0:25 – 0:35 (Dip): The first 10 minutes of hands-on time often look like chaos or confusion. This is normal. Participants are orienting to the task, figuring out what to do. Resist the urge to jump in immediately — give people 3–4 minutes to sort themselves out before you start roving. Premature intervention creates learned helplessness.

0:35 – 0:50 (Flow): If the exercise is working, most people will be in heads-down mode. Energy is low-visible but high-actual. Your job here is to be available without being disruptive. Walk slowly. Make eye contact. Ask "How's it going?" not "Do you need help?" — the second question implies they should need help.

0:50 – 1:00 (Close): Energy needs a deliberate lift coming out of the hands-on. Your transition line matters here. Move physically — stand at the front, raise your voice slightly. The debrief should feel like a gathering, not a slow wind-down.

On silence: Silence during the hands-on is good. Silence after a question you ask is fine — count to five before doing anything. The instinct to fill silence is usually wrong. Let people think.

When to push pace: When you are running behind and a concept is already landed — more explanation will not help. When the hands-on energy is high and participants do not need more context, just more time.

When to slow down: When multiple people have the same confused look. When a question surfaces a genuine gap in the framing you gave. When someone shares a debrief insight that deserves a moment — do not rush past a good insight for the sake of the clock.

11. Post-Session Debrief (for the Facilitator)

Block 20 minutes immediately after the session to capture these while the room is still fresh.

What to write down: - Which concept generated the most questions? Was it the same question more than once? That is a gap in the framing. - Which stage of the hands-on created the most confusion? What was the specific sticking point? - Which participant types were present and how did they go? Was there a Perfectionist who got unstuck? A Skeptic who came around? Note what worked. - What questions did /eval-faq answer well? What did it not answer well or get wrong? Those are gaps to file. - What was the energy at minute 45 — heads down and working, or scattered? Why?

What to capture from participants: - If anyone produced an interesting eval dataset or a novel grader approach, ask if you can keep a copy — with their permission. These become future examples. - Note any questions that were too advanced or out of scope — they may be worth a follow-up session or resource.

How to improve the next run: - One thing to change in the setup instructions (the most common failure is setup issues that should have been caught in prereqs) - One thing to change in the task prompt for Stage B (if many participants seemed unsure which agent task to pick, the options need clearer framing) - One timing adjustment — which segment ran long and at whose expense?

12. First-Time Facilitator Reassurance

If this is your first time running this session, read this section.

What will definitely go wrong: - At least one participant's setup will not work and you will not know why immediately. - You will say something about evals and an expert in the room will know a better answer. - The demo will take longer than 10 minutes, or the live run will be slow. - At least one participant will be visibly disengaged and you will wonder what you did wrong.

All of this is normal. None of it means the session is failing.

Why it is fine: Participants are not here to see a perfect presentation. They are here to run an eval for the first time and leave with vocabulary they did not have before. That outcome is achievable even when setup breaks, even when the demo is imperfect, and even when one person is on their phone.

The one thing that matters most: Make sure every participant generates a complete eval test set and interprets results with the interpreter skill before the session ends. Not writes a perfect eval — generates a test set and sees interpreted results. That single experience — producing their own scenarios and seeing what the results mean — is the thing that makes the concepts concrete. Everything else in the session exists to make that moment possible.

If you get to 0:49 and several people have not generated their test set yet, drop everything else and make it happen. Say: "Generate your test set now — even if it's not perfect, having a real eval set is the outcome." An imperfect generated test set beats a beautiful plan that never got generated.

One last thing: You know this material. The session outline, the harness, the concepts — you have worked with these things in the real world. That is what the room is paying attention to. The question is not whether you can deliver a perfect facilitation — it is whether you can share what you actually know. You can.
